Know the enemy

6 threats we train
you to recognise

These are the most common attacks targeting South African internet users today. Recognition is your first line of defence.

📧

Phishing Emails

Fake emails pretending to be your bank, SARS, or a courier company — designed to steal your login details or personal information.

🛡️ Red flag: Urgent language, misspelled sender address, suspicious links
📱

WhatsApp Scams

"Hi Mom, I've broken my phone — please send me money to this new number." Scammers impersonate family or friends to demand urgent transfers.

🛡️ Always call the person on their known number to verify
💼

Fake Job Offers

High-paying jobs requiring upfront "registration fees" or your ID and banking details. You pay them — and never hear from them again.

🛡️ Legitimate employers never ask for payment to get a job
🛒

Online Shopping Scams

Fake Facebook Marketplace listings, counterfeit websites offering luxury goods at impossible prices, and "delivery fee" scams.

🛡️ Use PayFast or PayPal — never EFT to strangers
💕

Romance Scams

Fake online relationships where the "partner" builds trust over weeks, then invents a crisis requiring an urgent money transfer.

🛡️ Never send money to someone you've only met online
📈

Investment Fraud

"Double your money in 30 days." Fake crypto platforms, pyramid schemes, and celebrity-endorsed investment scams targeting life savings.

🛡️ Verify with the FSCA before investing any money

⏱ 45 Minutes ✦ Beginner

Understanding Online Threats

You can't protect yourself from something you don't recognise. This opening module shows you what online attacks actually look like — using real examples that are circulating right now.

What you'll learn

  • What phishing is and how to spot it
  • WhatsApp scam patterns and scripts
  • How fake job ads are structured
  • Social engineering tactics scammers use
  • The psychology behind why scams work
  • Live activity: "Would you click this?"

📖 Lessons

1
What is phishing and why does it work?
▼

Phishing is when a criminal sends you a fake message pretending to be someone you trust — your bank, SARS, or a courier company. The message tricks you into clicking a link that either steals your password or installs software on your device.

Real Example

"Your FNB account has been suspended. Click here to verify your details immediately: www.fnb-secure-login.co.za"

⚠️FNB's real domain is fnb.co.za — anything else is fake. Your bank will NEVER ask you to verify details via a link in an SMS or email.

Phishing works because it creates urgency and fear. When you're panicking about a "suspended account," you stop thinking carefully and react quickly — exactly what the scammer wants.

2
WhatsApp scams: the "Wrong Number" trick
▼

WhatsApp scams are growing fast in South Africa. The most common types are the "family emergency" scam and the "wrong number" crypto scam.

Family Emergency Script

"Hi Dad, this is me on my friend's phone. I lost mine and I'm in trouble — I need R2,500 urgently for a taxi and food. Please EFT to this number: 082 XXX XXXX. I'll pay you back tonight."

💡The simple fix: Call your family member on their REAL number before sending any money. The scammer can't answer that phone.

In the "wrong number" scam, an attractive stranger "accidentally" messages you, befriends you over days or weeks, then introduces you to a fake crypto investment platform where you "make money" — until they disappear with your deposit.

3
Spotting fake job offers
▼

Fake job scams target unemployed people with promises of high-paying work-from-home jobs. They collect your personal information (for "ID verification") and often charge a registration or training fee.

  • The job pays unrealistically well for minimal skills
  • You're asked to pay a "registration" or "background check" fee
  • They ask for your ID number, banking details upfront
  • The "company" has no verifiable physical address
  • Interviews happen only on WhatsApp or Telegram
💡Search the company name + "scam" on Google. Check the CIPC website to see if the business is registered.

🎯 Quick Check

You receive an SMS: "SARS owes you R4,200 tax refund. Click here to claim: www.sars-refunds.net" — What should you do?

⏱ 45 Minutes ✦ Beginner

Password & Account Protection

Your password is your key. A weak password is like leaving your front door unlocked. This module teaches you to create strong passwords and add a second lock with Multi-Factor Authentication.

What you'll learn

  • Why "123456" is dangerous
  • How hackers guess passwords
  • The strong password formula
  • What MFA is and why it's critical
  • LIVE: Set up 2FA on Facebook
  • LIVE: Use Google Authenticator

📖 Lessons

1
How hackers crack your password
▼

Hackers use three main methods to steal passwords. Understanding them makes strong passwords make more sense.

  • Dictionary attack: A program tries every word in a dictionary, plus common variations like "password1" or "abc123"
  • Credential stuffing: Your email and password from an old data breach is tried on your bank, email, and social media
  • Social engineering: They guess using your name, birth date, or pet's name — information you shared publicly online
⚠️The most common South African passwords are: "password", "123456", your name + birth year (e.g. "sipho1990"), and your ID number. Never use these.
2
The strong password formula
▼

A strong password doesn't have to be hard to remember. Use the Three Random Words method:

Example

Think of 3 unrelated words: Purple · Bicycle · Mountain
Combine them: PurpleBicycle!Mountain7
This is 23 characters long, includes uppercase, symbols, and a number — and it's still memorable.

💡Golden rules: 12+ characters. Mix uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols. Never reuse a password across different sites. Use a different password for your email than for everything else — your email is the master key to all your other accounts.
3
Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): your second lock
▼

MFA means that even if someone steals your password, they still can't get in because they need a second piece of proof — usually a code sent to your phone or generated by an app.

Think of it like a two-door entrance: the first door is your password, the second door is a code only you can get.

  • Go to Facebook Settings → Security and Login → Two-Factor Authentication
  • Choose "Authentication App" (more secure than SMS)
  • Download Google Authenticator from your app store
  • Scan the QR code shown on Facebook
  • Enter the 6-digit code to confirm setup
💡Enable MFA on: your email, Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and your banking app — these accounts hold the most value for attackers.

⏱ 45 Minutes ✦ Beginner

Phone & Laptop Protection

Your phone and laptop are treasure chests of personal information. This module shows you simple habits that dramatically reduce your risk of being hacked through your devices.

What you'll learn

  • Why software updates are essential
  • Official app stores vs unknown sources
  • Screen locks and biometric security
  • iPhone security settings walkthrough
  • Android security settings walkthrough
  • Windows Defender and safe browsing

📖 Lessons

1
Why software updates protect you
▼

Every app and operating system has hidden weaknesses called "vulnerabilities." When these are discovered, hackers can use them to break into your device without you doing anything wrong. Updates patch these holes.

💡Rule: When your phone says "Software Update Available" — install it today, not next week. Turn on automatic updates so you never miss one.
  • iPhone: Settings → General → Software Update → Enable Automatic Updates
  • Android: Settings → System → System Update → Check for Updates
  • Windows: Start → Settings → Update & Security → Windows Update
2
Only install apps from official stores
▼

Apps from WhatsApp links, websites, or email attachments can contain malware — software that spies on you, steals your banking details, or locks your files.

⚠️Someone sends you a WhatsApp message: "Download this app to win R500!" — never install apps from links. Always go to the Google Play Store or Apple App Store directly.
💡Android users: Go to Settings → Security → and make sure "Install unknown apps" is turned OFF for all applications. This prevents accidental installations.
3
Screen locks and what they protect
▼

If your phone is lost or stolen and has no lock screen, the thief has immediate access to your emails, WhatsApp, photos, banking apps, and stored passwords.

  • Use a 6-digit PIN minimum — avoid 0000, 1234, your birth year
  • Enable fingerprint or Face ID as a convenient second option
  • Set auto-lock to 30 seconds or 1 minute
  • On iPhone: enable "Erase Data" after 10 failed attempts
  • On Android: enable Find My Device so you can remotely wipe it

⏱ 45 Minutes ✦ Intermediate

Protecting Your Money Online

Financial cybercrime is the fastest-growing threat in South Africa. This module covers the tactics criminals use to steal your money and gives you practical defences for every situation.

What you'll learn

  • Safe online and mobile banking habits
  • What an OTP is and how to protect it
  • SIM swap fraud: what it is and how to prevent it
  • ATM skimming and shoulder surfing
  • Fake investment scams (crypto, forex)
  • What to do if your account is drained

📖 Lessons

1
Safe banking: 7 rules that protect your money
▼
  • Only use your bank's official app — download from the App Store, not a link
  • Never do online banking on public Wi-Fi (coffee shops, malls, airports)
  • Log out of banking apps after every session — never stay logged in
  • Never share your OTP with anyone — not even bank staff
  • Set up transaction notification SMS alerts for every payment
  • Set a daily transaction limit lower than your full balance
  • Check your statements weekly for unfamiliar transactions
💡Your bank will NEVER call you and ask for your card PIN, OTP, or account password. If someone claims to be from your bank and asks for these — hang up and call the number on the back of your card.
2
SIM swap fraud: how criminals steal your number
▼

SIM swap fraud is when a criminal convinces your mobile network to transfer your phone number to a SIM card they control. Once they have your number, they intercept your bank OTPs and drain your accounts.

⚠️Warning signs: Your phone suddenly shows "No Service" for an unexplained period. You stop receiving calls and SMS messages. Check your bank account immediately and call your network provider.
  • Never share your ID number, date of birth, or address online
  • Add a SIM swap PIN/block at your mobile provider's store
  • Use an authentication app for MFA instead of SMS-based OTPs
  • If your phone loses signal unexpectedly, call your network immediately
3
Fake investments: how to spot a scam
▼

Fake investment scams promise unrealistically high returns with "no risk." They use celebrity faces (Elon Musk, Trevor Noah, famous businesspeople) in fake ads to appear legitimate.

Classic Red Flags

"Earn R15,000/month from home with just R500 investment." "Guaranteed 300% returns in 60 days." "Limited spots — act NOW." Celebrity testimonials on social media ads.

💡Before investing ANY money, verify the company on the FSCA website (fsca.co.za). All legitimate financial service providers must be registered. If they're not listed — it's a scam.

🎯 Quick Check

A man calls claiming to be from your bank. He says your account is under attack and he needs your OTP to "secure it." What do you do?

⏱ 30 Minutes ✦ Beginner

Social Media & Privacy

Everything you share publicly on social media is a tool criminals can use to target you. This module shows you how to lock down your profiles and limit what strangers can see about you.

What you'll learn

  • How identity theft happens online
  • LIVE: Make your Facebook profile private
  • Remove your phone number from public view
  • Limit who can see your posts and contact you
  • TikTok and LinkedIn privacy settings
  • What NOT to post on social media

📖 Lessons

1
How criminals use your public profile against you
▼

A public Facebook profile can give criminals your full name, photo, birthday, city, employer, family members' names, and phone number. They use this to impersonate you, answer security questions, or craft personalised scam messages.

⚠️Never post: your ID number, passport, bank card, boarding passes, house keys or location, details about when you'll be away from home.
💡Search your own name on Google right now. Everything that appears is what a criminal can see. We'll fix this together in class.
2
Facebook privacy settings: step by step
▼
  • Settings → Privacy → "Who can see your future posts?" → Set to Friends
  • Settings → Privacy → "Limit who can see past posts" → Limit Past Posts
  • Settings → Profile and Tagging → "Who can see your phone number?" → Only Me
  • Settings → Privacy → "Who can send you friend requests?" → Friends of Friends
  • Settings → Privacy → "Allow search engines to link to your profile?" → Off
  • Review any apps connected to your Facebook account and remove ones you don't use
3
LinkedIn and TikTok: platforms people forget to secure
▼

LinkedIn is a professional network but it's also a goldmine for criminals. Your employer, job title, skills, and connections can be used to craft convincing phishing emails targeting you or your company.

  • LinkedIn: Settings → Visibility → turn off "Profile viewing options" to anonymous
  • LinkedIn: Remove your phone number from your profile entirely
  • TikTok: Settings → Privacy → set account to Private
  • TikTok: Disable "Suggest your account to others"
💡On any platform: if you wouldn't shout it in a crowded mall — don't post it publicly online.

⏱ 30 Minutes ✦ Critical

What To Do If You're Hacked

Being hacked is terrifying — but how you respond in the first 30 minutes determines how much damage is done. This module gives you a clear, step-by-step emergency plan to follow.

What you'll learn

  • The 6-step Emergency Response Plan
  • How to change passwords while compromised
  • When and how to contact your bank
  • How to report hacked social media accounts
  • How to scan your device for malware
  • How to report cybercrime in South Africa

📖 Emergency Response Plan

1
STEP 1 & 2: Don't panic — change passwords immediately
▼

Panic causes mistakes. Take a breath, then act fast. Start with the most critical accounts first: your email (it controls everything else), then banking, then social media.

⚠️If you use the same password on multiple sites — change ALL of them. The hacker has already tried your password everywhere.
  • Change your email password first — this is your master account
  • Check if your email sent messages you didn't write — this confirms the hack
  • Change banking app password and PIN immediately
  • Change social media passwords
  • Use a device you trust — if your laptop is compromised, use your phone
2
STEP 3 & 4: Enable MFA — contact your bank
▼

After changing passwords, immediately enable Multi-Factor Authentication on every account you changed. This prevents the hacker from getting back in even if they captured your new password.

If any financial fraud has occurred or you suspect your banking details were compromised:

  • Call the number on the back of your bank card — do this NOW
  • Ask them to freeze all transactions while you investigate
  • Ask them to block any new payees from being added
  • Request a list of recent transactions to identify unauthorised payments
  • Get a reference number for your complaint — you'll need it
💡South African banking fraud numbers: FNB: 087 575 9406 | Absa: 0800 111 155 | Standard Bank: 0800 020 600 | Nedbank: 0800 110 929 | Capitec: 0860 10 20 43
3
STEP 5 & 6: Report the account — scan your device
▼

If your social media account is hacked, report it to the platform to freeze or recover it before the hacker changes the email and locks you out.

  • Facebook: facebook.com/hacked → "My account was hacked"
  • Instagram: instagram.com/hacked → Follow recovery steps
  • WhatsApp: Email [email protected] with your phone number

Then scan your device to remove any malware that enabled the hack:

  • iPhone: Update iOS → Apple devices rarely need third-party antivirus
  • Android: Install Malwarebytes (free) from the Play Store and run a full scan
  • Windows: Open Windows Security → Virus & threat protection → Full Scan
  • Report cybercrime to the SAPS Cybercrime Centre: 012 421 5494
